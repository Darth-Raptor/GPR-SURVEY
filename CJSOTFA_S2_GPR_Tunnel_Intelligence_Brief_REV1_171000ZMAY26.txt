1. EXECUTIVE ASSESSMENT

CJSOTF-A S2 assesses that the GPR survey identified a probable enemy subsurface network in the Candaldari District scan area. The strongest detected feature is a southwest-to-northeast corridor running from the E1 vicinity at grid 2455-1012, through the E2 vicinity at grid 2537-0993, toward the E3 vicinity at grid 2513-1094. This corridor is assessed as the primary tunnel axis and is likely suitable for concealed movement, evasion, limited storage, and protected transit between known access areas.

Satellite thermal imaging collected at 170400ZMAY26 increases confidence in the assessment by identifying thermal anomalies at E1 and E3 consistent with air exchange, concealed access, or recent use. Moderate thermal indicators near E2 and suspected node areas along the southern/eastern portion of the corridor suggest possible ventilation, junction, or branch infrastructure. These additional indicators are not assessed as confirmed entrances.

Bottom line: the E1-E2-E3 corridor should be treated as a probable operational tunnel system. E1, E2, and E3 are priority exploitation/control points. Other detected features should be handled as suspected subsurface nodes or unresolved anomalies pending ground confirmation.

2. OPERATIONAL CONTEXT

The survey was requested following previous reporting of enemy tunnel and cave use in the Candaldari area. The enemy has demonstrated the ability to use underground features for concealment, movement, defensive positioning, and materiel storage. Prior SSE recovered communications radios, thermal optics, night-vision equipment, and maps from an enemy cave system, indicating that local underground infrastructure may support more than simple evasion.

The detected network sits inside a compact 4.2 square kilometer scan area bounded approximately by grids 2400-0924 and 2600-1134. This places the assessed network within usable distance of concealed surface approaches, compounds, terrain folds, and possible militant support areas.

3. KEY JUDGMENTS

A. The E1-E2-E3 axis is the primary detected tunnel corridor.

The strongest GPR return traces an irregular corridor from grid 2455-1012 through grid 2537-0993 toward grid 2513-1094. This axis is assessed as the most likely continuous man-made underground movement route.

B. E1 and E3 are the strongest fused access indicators.

Both points correlate with strong GPR indicators and thermal anomalies. These locations should be treated as priority entry/exit or air-exchange candidates until ground exploitation determines exact function.

C. E2 is a priority control point along the primary corridor.

E2 sits along the assessed primary corridor and has moderate thermal support. It may represent an access point, junction, ventilation feature, or corridor-control location. It should be observed or secured during any exploitation of E1 or E3.

D. Additional features are suspected nodes, not known entrances.

The survey detected several possible branch or node areas outside E1/E2/E3. These are assessed as possible ventilation, branch, disturbed-overburden, or inactive tunnel features. They should not be briefed as confirmed entrances until physically verified.

E. The network is not fully mapped.

Several weak returns remain unresolved. This may reflect incomplete collection resolution, masked entrances, lower-use tunnel sections, natural/drainage clutter, or branches not currently active.

6. ENEMY USE ASSESSMENT

The detected network is assessed as suitable for the following enemy uses:

A. Concealed movement between access points.

The E1-E2-E3 corridor likely allows personnel to move beneath or around surface observation and defensive positions.

B. Evasion and escape from raid pressure.

Multiple suspected node areas increase the possibility that enemy personnel can break contact, bypass cordons, or exploit unobserved surface openings.

C. Cache and staging support.

Branch or node areas may support temporary storage of weapons, radios, medical supplies, explosives, or sustainment items.

D. Defensive ambush preparation.

Access points and suspected branch nodes near compounds, terrain folds, or concealed surface routes may allow enemy fighters to reposition or emerge outside the expected assault lane.

E. Command/courier movement.

The network may support clandestine courier movement or short-duration concealment for local facilitators.

7. THREAT IMPLICATIONS

A. Direct action against any single point may be insufficient.

If the network includes branches or unconfirmed exits, an assault or cordon focused only on E1 may allow personnel to escape toward E2, E3, or suspected node areas.

B. EOD support is required.

Any discovered entrance, shaft, or suspicious node should be treated as potentially booby-trapped, blocked, rigged, or prepared for denial.

C. GPR-negative and thermal-negative areas cannot be assumed clear.

Low-confidence or unresolved returns may still include inactive, masked, deeper, or naturally shielded tunnel sections.

D. Cordons should account for E1, E2, E3, and A-02/A-04.

These locations should be controlled or observed during any ground exploitation of the primary corridor.

E. Capture value may exceed destruction value.

If the network is intact, SSE may produce documents, communications equipment, explosive materials, biometric traces, tunnel maps, or detainees with knowledge of associated cells.
